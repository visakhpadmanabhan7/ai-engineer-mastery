Module 11 · Interview Mastery Day 68 120 min

Capstone — a full mock loop

This is the finale. Sixty-seven days of knowledge meet the one thing that actually gets offers: performing it under interview conditions, end to end, in one sitting. Today you run the whole loop, score yourself honestly, and find the two areas to sharpen before the real thing.

By the end you can
  • Run a complete four-round interview loop solo, using Claude as the interviewer.
  • Perform under back-to-back pressure, the way a real on-site actually feels.
  • Score each round on a concrete rubric and total a readiness signal.
  • Identify your two weakest areas and route back to the exact days that fix them.
  • Walk into the real loop knowing your structure holds.

How the capstone works

A real interview loop is four conversations in a row, and the difficulty is not any single one, it is doing all four while tired, without your notes, holding structure when you would rather ramble. So that is exactly how you will practice today. Block two uninterrupted hours. Run the four rounds in order, out loud, with Claude playing each interviewer. Do not pause to look things up mid-round; that is not available on the day. After each round, fill the scorecard while it is fresh.

Everything you need is below: which protocol prompt to use, what to paste, and what each round is testing. The protocol prompts live in Tools & Claude; #2 grades a single answer, #3 runs an interactive mock.

THE FULL LOOP — run in order, ~2 hours ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ ROUND 1 Recruiter screen ~15 min fit, arc, comms │ │ ROUND 2 Technical / AI deep-dive ~35 min depth, correctness │ │ ROUND 3 Live system design ~35 min structure, trade- │ │ offs, the clock │ │ ROUND 4 Behavioral ~25 min STAR, ownership │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ then: score all four → pick the 2 weakest → revisit those days → re-run only those rounds tomorrow

Round 1 · Recruiter screen (~15 min)

The recruiter screen is not technical. It tests communication, your career arc, salary/logistics alignment, and a basic "can this person talk like a colleague." It is also where many strong engineers get filtered for sounding unprepared on the easy questions.

Run it — protocol #3

Paste into Claude: "You are a recruiter doing a 15-minute phone screen for a mid-to-senior AI Engineer role in Germany. Ask me, one at a time: a quick intro / walk me through your background, why you are looking, why this kind of role, what you know about working with LLMs day to day, and salary expectations. Keep it conversational, push gently if an answer is vague, and at the end tell me whether you would pass me to the hiring manager and why." Answer out loud. Lead with your career arc from Day 67.

What good looks like

A crisp 75-second arc that ends pointed at the role; a "why looking" answer that runs toward the opportunity, not away from the current job; and a calm, specific read on what AI engineering involves day to day. No rambling, no apologizing, no "um, I guess."

Round 2 · Technical / AI-engineering deep-dive (~35 min)

This is the depth round: RAG, agents, evaluation, serving, the real mechanics. The interviewer drills until they hit the edge of your knowledge, so the test is not just correctness but how you handle the follow-up that goes one level deeper than you are comfortable.

Run it — protocol #3

Paste: "You are a senior AI engineer running a 35-minute technical interview. Ask me one question at a time across RAG, agents, evaluation, and LLM serving. After each answer, ask one harder follow-up that probes the edge of what I said. Do not show me answers. Cover at least: a RAG failure-mode question, an agent design-decision question, an LLM-as-judge question, and a cost/latency optimization question. At the end, score me 1-5 on correctness, depth, and how I handled the follow-ups." Answer out loud, and when you hit your edge, say so honestly and reason from first principles rather than bluffing.

What good looks like

Correct one-liners that expand into real depth on the follow-up; naming trade-offs unprompted ("I would rerank, accepting the latency cost"); and grounding answers in your MaiQ work where it fits. Handling "go deeper" gracefully, even admitting an edge and reasoning forward, scores higher than a confident wrong answer.

Round 3 · Live system design (~35 min)

Now you run the Day 66 playbook for real, under time, talking the whole way. The test is structure: do you clarify before you draw, put numbers on it, deep-dive the bottleneck, and name a trade-off at every fork.

Run it — protocol #3

Paste: "You are a staff engineer running a 35-minute system-design interview. Give me ONE AI/GenAI design prompt (your choice: a multi-tenant RAG assistant, an LLM gateway, an agent platform, or a semantic search service). Then play the interviewer: do not let me skip requirements or numbers, push back if I jump to architecture, and ask for trade-offs if I do not volunteer them. At the end, score me 1-5 on each of: requirements, estimates, architecture, depth of deep-dive, trade-offs, and operations." Sketch on paper. Open with the clarifying sentence. Run the six phases.

What good looks like

You scope before you draw, drop at least one real number, say "the bottleneck here is X so I will deep-dive that," and state a trade-off ("I am trading Y for Z because...") at every choice. You sweep the 11-point checklist near the end and volunteer your own design's risks. The clock never runs you; you run it.

Round 4 · Behavioral (~25 min)

The closer. Your five STAR stories and your set-pieces, under the social pressure of "tell me about a time...". The test is proportions, ownership, honesty, and ending on learnings.

Run it — protocol #3

Paste: "You are a hiring manager running a 25-minute behavioral interview. Ask me, one at a time: tell me about something you shipped, a time something broke in production, a disagreement with a teammate, a decision you made without enough data, and finally why this company. After each, ask one probing follow-up. Score each answer 1-5 on STAR structure (was it Action-heavy?), ownership ('I' vs 'we'), a concrete result, and a learning." Answer out loud, map each prompt to one of your five stories, and watch your "we" count.

What good looks like

Each story hits the proportions (Action over half), says "I" for your decisions, lands a real number, and ends on a transferable lesson. The "why this company" answer fails the copy-paste test. No hero narratives, one honest stumble per failure story.

Reading your total

This is a rough self-signal, not a verdict. 80+: you are interview-ready; keep the bank warm and go apply. 65–79: solid, with one or two soft rounds; target those before a real loop. Under 65: do not panic, you have the knowledge; you need reps on delivery. Re-run the weakest rounds every other day for a week and watch the number climb. The score that matters is the trend across repeated mock loops, not any single run.

The loop back into the path

Now make the capstone actually useful: take your two lowest-scoring areas and route straight back to the days that fix them. This is the whole point of scoring, to aim your remaining prep instead of re-reviewing everything.

Pick exactly two, revisit them today or tomorrow, and re-run only those rounds. Then run the full loop again in a few days with a fresh prompt. Two or three full loops, spaced, is what turns "I know this" into "I can perform this on demand."

Practice — the capstone itself is the practice

Today there is one assignment, and it is the whole loop. These prompts help you run and debrief it well.

Problem 1

You finish the full loop and your total is a 72, dragged down by Round 3 (system design, 22/35) and Round 1 (recruiter, 9/15). What is your exact plan for the next 48 hours?

Show solution

Route to the two specific fixes, do not re-review everything. For Round 3, reopen Day 66 and re-drill the time-boxed structure and the two seniority phrases ("the bottleneck here is X" and "I am trading Y for Z"), then run two fresh system-design mocks back to back tomorrow, because that round's weakness is almost always structure under the clock, which only reps fix. For Round 1, reopen Day 67 and rehearse the career arc out loud five times until it is effortless, since a 9/15 recruiter score is usually a rusty arc, not a knowledge gap. Then, two days out, run the full loop again with new prompts and confirm both rounds climbed. The plan is targeted reps on the two weak rounds plus one full re-run to verify, not a vague "study more."

Problem 2

During the technical round you froze on a question you actually know, then nailed an easier one right after. In a real loop, freezing happens. What is the in-the-moment recovery, and how do you train it?

Show solution

In the moment: buy time out loud instead of going silent. "Let me think about that for a second" is completely acceptable and far better than a panicked non-answer; then anchor to first principles you do know ("okay, an LLM is a next-token predictor, so...") and build forward from there. Freezing is a state problem, not a knowledge problem, and the cure is reps under pressure, which is exactly why this capstone exists. The more cold mock questions you have faced (Day 65's uncued drills and these loops), the less the freeze fires, because the situation stops being novel. Train it by deliberately running mocks when you are a little tired, since that is the real on-site condition.

A few final questions, mixing everything

A grab-bag across the whole path. If all of these come out clean and cold, you are ready.

  • Explain how an LLM works, then pivot to why you obsess over retrieval and evaluation.
    Key: next-token prediction, lossy parametric memory; that mechanism is exactly why grounding (RAG) and measurement (eval) are non-optional.
  • Design a multi-tenant RAG assistant, end to end.
    Key: clarify + estimate first; ingestion → hybrid retrieval + rerank + data-layer tenant filter → grounded generation with citations → eval + tracing + cost; sweep the 11 points.
  • When would you NOT reach for an agent, and how do you eval one if you do?
    Key: skip agents for small/scoped/deterministic/latency-tight tasks; eval = final-answer correctness + tool-call accuracy + trajectory, plus tracing.
  • Tell me about an AI system you shipped and what broke first.
    Key: Action-heavy STAR; the retrieval-cost work or the debugging story; a ruled-out alternative, a real number, a process learning.
  • How do you cut LLM cost without losing quality, and how do you prove it?
    Key: prompt caching, routing, lower top-k, smaller generator; prove with an LLM-as-judge harness against a quality floor (you did: 37% for a 2% drop).
  • Why AI engineering, why now, and why this company?
    Key: forward arc (data science → JURIX research turn → MaiQ production) ending at the role; one researched specific that fails the copy-paste test.
  • What is the strongest reason I might NOT want to join? (you, to the founder)
    Key: the anti-pitch; asking for the honest downside signals a clear-eyed candidate and gets you the real risks.

Build it (your standing practice)

Keep the loop alive

Save your four protocol-#3 mock prompts (recruiter, technical, design, behavioral) in a notes file as your reusable interview gym. Before any real on-site, run the matching round once the day before. Schedule a full loop every week while you are actively interviewing, each time with a different design prompt and different behavioral angles, and keep the rolling scorecard so you can see the trend. The bank from Day 65 stays your daily five-minute warm-up. Tools and the protocol prompts: Tools & Claude.

Connect it to your work

You are interviewing from strength

Walk in remembering what you actually carry. You ship production AI on MaiQ, you built an LLM-as-judge harness and cut inference cost 37% for a 2% quality drop, you run hybrid retrieval into pgvector behind a FastAPI service, and you shipped a Skill Store that lets users build their own agents in chat, a genuinely rare credential. Behind that sits a JURIX 2024 Best Student Paper and a Grade 1.4 with Best Outgoing Student. Most candidates are reciting concepts; you have operated them. The capstone is not there to find out whether you know enough, you do. It is there to make sure the room hears it.

You are ready — keep the bank alive

Sixty-eight days ago an LLM was a next-token predictor you could define; now you can design the systems around it, evaluate them, serve them at cost, and tell the story of building them under interview pressure. The knowledge is done. From here it is reps: keep the question bank warm five minutes a day, run a full mock loop each week you are interviewing, and route every weak round back to the day that fixes it. Trust your trajectory, lead with your real work, and go get the offer. You are ready.
