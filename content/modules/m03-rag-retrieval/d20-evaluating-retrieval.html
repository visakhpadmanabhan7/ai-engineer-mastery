Module 3 · RAG & Retrieval Day 20 55 min

Evaluating retrieval

Everything in this module, chunking, hybrid search, reranking, query transformation, is a guess until you can measure it. Today you build the discipline that separates engineers who tune RAG from engineers who tweak it: a golden set, the right metrics, and a path from offline numbers to a safe online rollout. This is the heart of your edge.

By the end you can
  • Build a golden set of (query, ideal_answer, ideal_chunks) and explain why each field exists.
  • Compute and interpret recall@k, MRR, and NDCG, and say which one you trust most and why.
  • Separate retrieval evaluation from generation evaluation, and stop conflating the two failures.
  • Name the generation-side metrics (faithfulness, answer relevancy) and where they belong.
  • Walk the full path from offline golden set to shadow/interleave to a guarded online A/B.

1 · You cannot improve what you do not measure

Here is the trap. You swap a chunking strategy, eyeball five queries, decide it "feels better," and ship. Next week answers regress on a query family you never looked at, and you have no way to know which change caused it. RAG has too many knobs, chunk size, overlap, embedder, top-k, fusion weights, reranker, query transforms, for intuition to track. Evaluation turns each of those from a vibe into a number you can move on purpose and defend in review.

The first commitment is the most important and the most skipped: build a golden set, a fixed, curated collection of questions with known-correct answers and known-correct source chunks, that you run every candidate change against. Without it, every "improvement" is a story; with it, every improvement is a measured delta.

2 · The golden set: (query, ideal_answer, ideal_chunks)

Each golden example carries three fields, and the third is the one beginners omit and seniors insist on:

  • query, a realistic question, ideally mined from real user logs, not invented at your desk.
  • ideal_answer, the correct response a human would accept. This is for evaluating generation.
  • ideal_chunks, the specific source passages that contain the answer. This is for evaluating retrieval, independently of what the generator does with them.

That separation is the whole point. If you only store ideal answers, a bad final answer tells you something broke but not where, retrieval or generation. By labeling which chunks should have been retrieved, you can ask a sharper question: "did the right chunk make it into the top-k at all?" That is a pure retrieval question, answerable without ever calling the generator.

# A golden set is just data. Start with 30-50 hand-labeled examples;
# grow it by mining production queries that failed.
golden = [
  {
    "query": "what is the webhook retry limit on the Pro plan?",
    "ideal_answer": "Pro retries failed webhooks up to 5 times over 24h.",
    "ideal_chunks": ["doc_42#webhooks-retry", "doc_42#plan-limits"],
  },
  # … 30-50+ of these, tagged by intent & difficulty
]

Two practical notes. Tag each example by intent and difficulty so you can read metrics per slice (a change that helps simple lookups may hurt multi-hop). And keep the set alive: a golden set that never absorbs new failures slowly stops representing real traffic, the curation discipline you will meet again in Module 5.

3 · Recall@k: the metric that matters most

Recall@k answers the single most important retrieval question: did we retrieve the relevant chunk(s) at all, somewhere in the top k? It is the recall ceiling for everything downstream, because a reranker can only reorder what was retrieved, and a generator can only ground in what it was given. If the right chunk is not in the top-k, no amount of clever prompting or reranking can save the answer. That is why recall@k is the metric you optimize first and trust most.

recall@k = (# of ideal chunks found in the top-k) / (total ideal chunks) query: "Pro plan webhook retry limit" ideal_chunks = {A, B} retrieved top-5: [ C, A, D, E, F ] A found, B missed recall@5 = 1/2 = 0.50 ← B never made it in: hard ceiling on quality fix the recall miss FIRST (chunking? embedder? hybrid? wider k?), THEN worry about ordering.

Watch the k. recall@20 tells you whether the chunk is reachable at all (a retrieval-and-indexing question); recall@5 tells you whether it survives into the small window you actually feed the generator (a ranking question). A common diagnosis pattern: recall@20 is high but recall@5 is low, your retriever finds the chunk but ranks it poorly, which is precisely the gap a reranker (Day 18) closes. Read both.

4 · MRR and NDCG: when order and grades matter

Recall@k ignores where in the list the relevant chunk landed. Two more metrics add that.

MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank) rewards getting a relevant chunk high. For each query it takes 1 / (rank of the first relevant result), then averages over queries. First-position hits score 1.0, second 0.5, third 0.33. It is the right lens when one good chunk is enough and you care that it surfaces near the top (e.g. a single-answer lookup).

MRR = mean over queries of 1 / (rank of first relevant result) q1: first relevant at rank 1 → 1/1 = 1.00 q2: first relevant at rank 3 → 1/3 = 0.33 q3: first relevant at rank 2 → 1/2 = 0.50 MRR = (1.00 + 0.33 + 0.50) / 3 = 0.61

NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) is the most complete: it handles graded relevance (a chunk can be "perfect = 3", "useful = 1", "irrelevant = 0", not just yes/no) and applies a logarithmic discount so a relevant chunk lower in the list contributes less. DCG sums each result's gain divided by log2(rank + 1); you then normalize by the ideal ordering's DCG so the score sits in [0, 1] and is comparable across queries. Use NDCG when relevance is not binary and ranking quality across the whole list matters.

NDCG: graded gains, log-discounted by position, then normalized. retrieved ranks → 1 2 3 4 relevance grade 3 0 2 1 discount 1/log2(r+1) /1 /1.58 /2 /2.32 DCG = 3/1 + 0/1.58 + 2/2 + 1/2.32 = 3 + 0 + 1.0 + 0.43 = 4.43 ideal order (3,2,1,0): IDCG = 3/1 + 2/1.58 + 1/2 = 3 + 1.27 + 0.5 = 4.77 NDCG = DCG / IDCG = 4.43 / 4.77 ≈ 0.93 ← strong ranking, not perfect
Interview-grade phrasing

"Recall@k asks did we retrieve it at all and is the ceiling on every downstream step, so I optimize it first. MRR rewards ranking the first relevant result high, good when one chunk suffices. NDCG is graded and log-discounted, the most complete when relevance has degrees. I separate retrieval metrics from generation metrics so a bad answer points at the right culprit, and I never trust an offline win until it survives a shadow run and a guarded A/B."

5 · Retrieval eval is not generation eval

A RAG answer can fail in two structurally different places, and collapsing them is the most common evaluation mistake.

  • Retrieval failure, the right chunk never reached the generator. The model answered from the wrong context, or from nothing. Caught by recall@k / MRR / NDCG against ideal_chunks. No generator needed.
  • Generation failure, the right chunk was retrieved, but the model ignored it, contradicted it, or padded the answer with unsupported claims. Caught by faithfulness and answer-relevancy against the produced answer.

The decision tree is liberating: was the ideal chunk in the top-k? If no, it is a retrieval problem, fix chunking, embeddings, hybrid weighting, or k, and do not touch the prompt. If yes but the answer is still wrong, it is a generation problem, fix the prompt, grounding instructions, or model. Evaluating them separately means every regression has a clear owner.

bad final answer │ ▼ ideal chunk in top-k? ├─ NO → RETRIEVAL problem → chunking / embedder / hybrid / k / rerank └─ YES → GENERATION problem → prompt / grounding / model │ ▼ measure faithfulness (did it stick to the context?) measure answer relevancy (did it address the question?)

6 · Generation-side metrics (forward ref to Module 5)

You will go deep on these in Module 5; here is the vocabulary so the boundary is clear. RAGAS-style, LLM-as-judge metrics score the generated answer:

  • Faithfulness, the one to trust most: is every claim in the answer supported by the retrieved context? This is the anti-hallucination metric, it catches the model inventing beyond its sources.
  • Answer relevancy: does the answer actually address the question, or does it wander?
  • Contextual precision/recall: of the retrieved chunks, how many were relevant, and were the relevant ones ranked well, a generation-eval framing that loops back to the retrieval metrics above.

The key boundary: retrieval metrics need only ideal_chunks and are cheap and deterministic; generation metrics need the produced answer and usually an LLM judge. Keep them in separate stages of your eval harness so a retrieval change is scored on retrieval metrics, not muddied by generator noise.

7 · Proving a change is better: offline → shadow → online

An offline win on your golden set is necessary but not sufficient, the golden set is a sample, and production is the whole distribution. Promote a change through three gates, each catching what the previous misses:

1. OFFLINE (golden set) fast, cheap, repeatable run candidate vs current, compare recall@k / MRR / NDCG per slice gate: does it win without regressing any slice? │ pass ▼ 2. SHADOW / INTERLEAVE real traffic, no user impact shadow: run new retriever alongside old on live queries, log both interleave: blend both lists, see which gets the click/citation catches: real query distribution, latency, failures offline missed │ pass ▼ 3. ONLINE A/B (per-user) the real verdict, with a safety net split users, compare outcome metrics, run to significance GUARDRAILS: latency p95, cost/query, faithfulness floor → auto-rollback

Offline on the golden set is your fast inner loop: cheap, deterministic, run on every change, and the gate is "wins without regressing any slice." Shadow mode runs the new retriever on real traffic without affecting users (log its results alongside the live one), exposing the real query distribution, real latency, and real failure modes your curated set never contained; interleaving blends old and new results into one list and sees which side earns the click or citation, a sensitive comparison with little risk. Online A/B is the final verdict: split users, compare outcome metrics, run to statistical significance, but never without guardrail metrics, latency p95/p99, cost per query, and a faithfulness floor, that trip an automatic rollback if the "better" retriever quietly made things slower, pricier, or less grounded. A retrieval win that doubles latency or halves faithfulness is not a win.

Worked example: a chunking change, scored honestly

Example

Hypothesis: switching from fixed 256-token chunks to parent-child chunking (Day 14) will raise retrieval quality. You run both against a 40-example golden set, tagged by intent.

metric (top-5) fixed-256 parent-child verdict recall@5 (overall) 0.78 0.86 ↑ retrieves more recall@5 (multi-hop) 0.55 0.72 ↑ big win on hard slice recall@5 (exact-lookup) 0.91 0.88 ↓ slight regression MRR 0.61 0.69 ↑ ranks first hit higher NDCG 0.70 0.77 ↑ better overall order → net win, but exact-lookup regressed. Inspect those queries: parent chunks dilute exact identifiers. Action: keep parent-child, but ensure BM25 (Day 15) is in the hybrid mix to protect exact-match. → THEN shadow on live traffic, watch latency (bigger parents = more tokens), THEN guarded A/B with a faithfulness floor.

Notice what the per-slice read bought you. The overall recall@5 went up, an honest engineer could have shipped on that alone, but the slice view exposed a real regression on exact-lookup queries that an aggregate number hid. That is not a reason to abandon the change; it is a reason to combine it with hybrid search so BM25 protects exact matches, then re-measure. The aggregate tells you whether; the slices tell you where, and the where is what you act on. This is exactly the loop that lets you run many experiments and keep only the changes that win without hidden costs.

Practice — do these before revealing

Say each answer out loud in full sentences, as if to an interviewer. Then open the solution and compare. The gap is your lesson.

Problem 1

Final answers are wrong on about 20% of queries. With your golden set, how do you decide whether to fix retrieval or fix the prompt, without guessing?

Show solution

Run the failing queries through retrieval alone and check recall@k against ideal_chunks: for each failure, was the ideal chunk actually in the top-k? Split the failures into two buckets. Where the ideal chunk was missing, it is a retrieval problem, fix chunking, embeddings, hybrid weighting, or widen k, and the prompt is irrelevant. Where the ideal chunk was present but the answer was still wrong, it is a generation problem, measure faithfulness and answer relevancy and fix the prompt, grounding instructions, or model. The labeled ideal chunks are what let you assign each failure an owner instead of guessing, and the relative size of the two buckets tells you where to spend first.

Problem 2

A change improves recall@20 but barely moves recall@5, and answers do not improve. What is the diagnosis, and what would you do, both to the pipeline and to which metric you watch?

Show solution

Higher recall@20 with flat recall@5 means the retriever now finds the relevant chunk but still ranks it outside the small window you actually feed the generator, so answer quality cannot improve because the generator never sees it. That is a ranking problem, not a retrieval-coverage problem, and the canonical fix is a reranker (Day 18) that rescores the top-20 candidates and lifts the relevant one into the top-5. Watch recall@5 (and MRR) as your gating metric after adding the reranker, since recall@20 was already telling you the chunk is reachable; the open question is whether it survives the precision cut.

Problem 3

Your new retriever wins clearly on the offline golden set. A teammate says "ship it." Make the case for what still has to happen, and what could make this "win" a production loss.

Show solution

The golden set is a curated sample, not the live distribution, so an offline win proves the change is promising, not safe. Promote it through two more gates: shadow or interleave on real traffic to expose the true query distribution, real latency, and failure modes the golden set never contained, then a per-user A/B run to statistical significance. Crucially, attach guardrail metrics, p95/p99 latency, cost per query, and a faithfulness floor, with automatic rollback, because a retriever that raises recall but doubles latency, inflates cost, or lowers groundedness is a net loss for users even though the offline number looked great. The win is real only when it survives real traffic without tripping a guardrail.

Interview questions for this topic

From your Notion bank's RAG & Retrieval slice and common 2026 screens. Drill the one-line keys, then expand each into a 90-second answer with Claude using protocol prompt #2.

  • What are recall@k, MRR, and NDCG?
    Key: recall@k = did we retrieve it at all (most important, the ceiling); MRR = 1/rank of first relevant; NDCG = graded, log-discounted, normalized.
  • How do you evaluate generation, not just retrieval?
    Key: RAGAS-style LLM-as-judge, faithfulness (trust most), answer relevancy, contextual precision/recall, scored on the produced answer.
  • How do you prove a retrieval change is actually better?
    Key: offline golden set (per-slice) → shadow/interleaving on live traffic → online per-user A/B with guardrail metrics and auto-rollback.
  • Why separate retrieval eval from generation eval?
    Key: a bad answer can come from a missing chunk or a misused chunk; labeling ideal_chunks lets you assign each failure an owner instead of guessing.
  • What goes into a golden set and how do you keep it useful?
    Key: (query, ideal_answer, ideal_chunks) tagged by intent/difficulty; mine real failed queries continuously so it keeps representing live traffic.
  • Which retrieval metric do you optimize first and why?
    Key: recall@k, because reranking and generation can only work with what was retrieved; if the chunk is not in top-k nothing downstream recovers it.
  • How do you cut RAG cost ~30% without quality loss?
    Key: embed once, cache reranks, route, lower top-k, smaller generator, prompt caching, measured on the golden set so quality is held (e.g. 37% cut at ~2% retrieval drop).

Build it (15–25 min)

Hands-on

Hand-write a tiny golden set: 8–10 questions over a handful of your docs, each with its ideal_chunks labeled. Write a ten-line Python function that, given a retriever's top-k for each query, computes recall@k and MRR across the set (just sets and a reciprocal-rank average, no library needed). Run it against two retrieval settings (e.g. dense-only vs hybrid) and print the deltas. Then skim the RAGAS metrics docs to see context-precision/recall and faithfulness formalized, you will implement these in Module 5.

Stretch: add per-intent tags to your examples and print recall@k per slice, then deliberately make one change that wins overall but regresses one slice, exactly the trap aggregate metrics hide.

Today's takeaways

A golden set of (query, ideal_answer, ideal_chunks) turns RAG tuning from vibes into measured deltas, and the labeled chunks are what let you separate retrieval failures from generation failures. Recall@k is the metric you optimize first and trust most, because it is the ceiling on everything downstream; MRR adds "rank the first hit high," NDCG adds graded, position-discounted scoring. Read metrics per slice, an aggregate win can hide a real regression. And never ship on an offline win alone: promote through offline, then shadow/interleave, then a guarded online A/B with latency, cost, and faithfulness guardrails. Measure, do not guess.
