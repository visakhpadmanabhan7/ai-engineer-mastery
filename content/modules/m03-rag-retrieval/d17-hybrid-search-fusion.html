Hybrid search & fusion

You have two retrievers with opposite blind spots: dense for meaning, BM25 for exact tokens. Today you combine them into one ranked list, and learn why Reciprocal Rank Fusion is the safe default for doing it.

By the end you can
  • Describe hybrid search: run BM25 and dense in parallel, then fuse the results.
  • Explain Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) and why being rank-only makes it scale-free.
  • Contrast RRF with weighted score fusion and say when each is right.
  • Argue why score-scale mismatch makes RRF the safe default.
  • Work an RRF example by hand to merge two ranked lists.

The idea: two retrievers, one list

Yesterday's punchline was that lexical and dense retrieval fail on opposite queries: BM25 misses paraphrase, dense misses exact identifiers. The obvious move is to stop choosing. Hybrid search runs both retrievers in parallel, BM25 over the keyword index and dense search over the vector index, and merges their two ranked lists into a single result. A query for an error code is rescued by BM25; a query phrased in synonyms is rescued by dense; and a chunk that both retrievers like rises to the top, which is a strong signal it is genuinely relevant.

The whole design question reduces to one thing: how do you merge two ranked lists into one? That merge step is called fusion, and getting it right is the substance of today.

HYBRID SEARCH user query ├──────────────► BM25 (lexical index) → ranked list A │ (exact tokens, codes, names) └──────────────► dense (vector index) → ranked list B (meaning, paraphrase) │ ▼ ╔══════════════╗ ║ FUSION ║ merge A + B → one ranked list ╚══════════════╝ (RRF, or weighted score fusion) │ ▼ top-k → (rerank, Day 18) → generate

1 · The merge problem: scores do not compare

Your first instinct is probably "just add the two scores." That fails, and understanding why is the key insight of the lesson. The two retrievers produce scores on completely different scales with different distributions:

  • A BM25 score is an unbounded positive number whose magnitude depends on term rarity, document length, and corpus statistics. A great match might score 12, or 30, depending on the corpus.
  • A cosine similarity is bounded in a narrow band (often roughly 0.6 to 0.9 for plausible matches), tightly clustered, and means something entirely different.

Add or average these directly and BM25's larger raw numbers swamp the cosine scores, so the dense retriever effectively stops contributing. The scales are incomparable, and the mismatch is not a tuning detail, it silently breaks the fusion.

Concept

The fusion trap is score-scale mismatch: BM25 scores and cosine similarities live on different, incomparable scales, so naively adding them lets one retriever dominate. You either normalize the scores carefully (fragile, corpus-dependent) or sidestep scores entirely and fuse on rank. The latter is RRF, and it is why RRF is the safe default.

2 · Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF)

RRF sidesteps the score problem with one elegant move: throw the scores away and fuse on rank position instead. Each document gets a contribution from each list based only on where it ranked, not what it scored. The formula is small and worth knowing exactly:

# For each document d, sum over the lists it appears in:
RRF(d) = sum over lists of  1 / (k + rank_in_that_list)

# rank is 1-based (best = 1). k is a constant, commonly ~60.
# Higher total = better. Documents in BOTH lists get two terms = a boost.

Read what each part does:

  • 1 / (k + rank) means rank 1 contributes the most and the value falls off smoothly as rank grows. A document near the top of either list gets a big contribution.
  • Summing across lists means a document that appears in both the BM25 and dense lists earns two contributions, so cross-retriever agreement is rewarded automatically, which is exactly the signal you want.
  • The constant k (commonly around 60) softens the influence of the very top ranks and controls how quickly the contribution decays. A larger k flattens the curve so lower-ranked items still matter; the value is robust and rarely needs tuning.
  • Rank-only, therefore scale-free. Because RRF never touches the raw scores, BM25's unbounded numbers and cosine's narrow band become irrelevant. It does not matter that one retriever scores in the tens and the other in tenths, only the ordering is used.
Interview-grade phrasing

"RRF fuses ranked lists by summing 1 over (k plus rank) across lists, with k around 60. Because it uses rank, not score, it is scale-free, so it sidesteps the score-scale mismatch between BM25 and cosine, and it rewards documents both retrievers agree on. That is why it is the default fusion method." Crisp and correct.

3 · Weighted score fusion, and when to prefer it

The alternative is weighted score fusion: normalize each retriever's scores onto a common range (say 0 to 1), then take a weighted sum, final = α · dense_norm + (1 - α) · bm25_norm. The weight α lets you dial how much each retriever matters, which is its advantage: if you know dense should dominate for your corpus, you can encode that.

The catch is the normalization, and it is a real one. To put scores on a common scale you typically min-max normalize per query, but the min and max shift from query to query and corpus to corpus, so the mapping is unstable and a few outlier scores can distort it. You are reintroducing exactly the score-scale fragility RRF was designed to avoid, now with a tuning parameter to babysit. Prefer weighted fusion only when you have a reliable way to normalize and a measured reason to weight the retrievers unequally, and you have the evaluation harness to tune α honestly.

4 · When hybrid helps most

Hybrid search is rarely worse than either retriever alone, but its uplift is largest in specific situations, worth naming so you can justify the added complexity:

  • Mixed query distributions. Real users mix conceptual questions with exact-identifier lookups in the same product. Neither retriever alone serves both; hybrid serves each query with whichever retriever is strong on it.
  • Domains heavy in names, codes, and jargon. Technical docs, legal text, medical records, support knowledge bases, anywhere exact tokens and meaning both matter, are where the two retrievers' strengths stack.
  • Recall-critical applications. When missing the right passage is costly, two retrievers cast a wider, complementary net than one, and fusion lifts the chunks they agree on.

It helps least when queries are uniform, an all-conceptual semantic-search product, or an all-exact log search, where one retriever already covers the distribution and the second adds latency for little gain. As always, measure: hybrid is the strong default, but Day 19's evaluation tells you whether it actually beat the single-retriever baseline on your data.

Worked example: fuse two ranked lists with RRF

Example

A query returns these two top-4 lists. Fuse them with RRF, using k = 60. Document D appears in both lists; watch it win.

BM25 list: 1. A 2. D 3. C 4. E Dense list: 1. B 2. D 3. A 4. F RRF(d) = Σ 1 / (60 + rank) [only lists where d appears] A: 1/(60+1) + 1/(60+3) = 0.01639 + 0.01587 = 0.03226 D: 1/(60+2) + 1/(60+2) = 0.01613 + 0.01613 = 0.03226 B: 1/(60+1) = 0.01639 C: 1/(60+3) = 0.01587 E: 1/(60+4) = 0.01563 F: 1/(60+4) = 0.01563 FUSED ranking: 1. A (0.03226) ┐ tie: top of one list (rank 1) ≈ middle of both 1. D (0.03226) ┘ D earns it by being rank 2 in BOTH retrievers 3. B (0.01639) 4. C (0.01587) ...

The lesson is in document D. It was never rank 1 in either list, yet it ties for the top of the fused list because both retrievers ranked it highly (rank 2 each). RRF rewarded that agreement automatically by summing two contributions. Meanwhile B, which was rank 1 in dense but absent from BM25, gets only one contribution and lands below. Notice we never used a single raw score, the BM25 numbers and cosine numbers never entered the math, only positions did. That is scale-free fusion in action: it would produce the identical result whether BM25 scored in the tens or the thousands.

Why this matters for you as an AI engineer

Three consequences, derived not memorized

Stop choosing retrievers ← BM25 and dense fail on opposite queries, so running both in parallel and fusing serves the whole query distribution one retriever alone cannot.
Fuse on rank, not score ← BM25 and cosine scores are incomparable, so RRF (rank-only, scale-free) sidesteps the mismatch and is the safe default; reach for weighted fusion only with reliable normalization.
Agreement is signal ← a chunk both retrievers rank highly is probably truly relevant, and RRF surfaces it automatically by summing contributions across lists.

Problem 1

An engineer combined BM25 and dense by adding their raw scores. Conceptual queries got noticeably worse after the change. Diagnose the bug and give the one-line fix.

Show solution

This is score-scale mismatch. BM25 scores are unbounded positive numbers that can run into the tens or higher, while cosine similarities sit in a narrow band around 0.6 to 0.9, so adding the raw scores lets BM25's large magnitudes swamp the cosine contribution. The dense retriever effectively stops mattering, which is exactly why the conceptual (paraphrase) queries, the ones that depend on dense, degraded. The one-line fix: fuse on rank instead of raw score using Reciprocal Rank Fusion (sum of 1/(k+rank), k around 60), which is scale-free and never compares the incomparable scores. If you genuinely need score-weighted fusion, you must min-max normalize each retriever's scores first and tune the weight, but RRF is the simpler, safer default.

Problem 2

Two ranked lists. List 1 (BM25): [X, Y, Z]. List 2 (dense): [Y, W, X]. Compute the RRF order with k = 60 and explain which document the method favors and why.

Show solution

Apply RRF(d) = sum of 1/(60 + rank) over the lists d appears in (1-based ranks):

  • Y: 1/(60+2) + 1/(60+1) = 0.01613 + 0.01639 = 0.03252 (in both lists, ranks 2 and 1)
  • X: 1/(60+1) + 1/(60+3) = 0.01639 + 0.01587 = 0.03226 (in both lists, ranks 1 and 3)
  • Z: 1/(60+3) = 0.01587 (BM25 only)
  • W: 1/(60+2) = 0.01613 (dense only)

Fused order: Y, X, W, Z. The method favors Y because it appears in both lists at high ranks (1 and 2), so it collects two large contributions, edging out X (also in both, but at ranks 1 and 3). Documents found by only one retriever (W, Z) fall below those both retrievers agree on, even though W was rank 2 in dense. RRF's core behavior: cross-retriever agreement, rewarded automatically through summation, beats a strong showing in a single list.

Problem 3

Your manager asks why you did not just use weighted score fusion with a tuned α, "it is more flexible." Give a balanced answer: when RRF is the right default, and the specific condition under which weighted fusion becomes worth it.

Show solution

RRF is the right default because it fuses on rank, so it is scale-free and immune to the score-scale mismatch between BM25 (unbounded) and cosine (narrow band), and it needs essentially no tuning, the constant k around 60 is robust. Weighted score fusion is genuinely more flexible (the weight α lets you make dense or BM25 count more), but that flexibility costs you a reliable score-normalization step, and per-query min-max normalization is unstable because the min and max shift across queries and corpora, reintroducing exactly the fragility RRF avoids, plus a parameter to babysit. The specific condition under which weighted fusion is worth it: when you have a reliable, stable normalization for both retrievers' scores and an evaluation harness that shows a measured uplift from weighting them unequally on your data. Absent that evidence, RRF is the safer, simpler choice, and you can always upgrade later once an eval justifies it.

Today's takeaways

Hybrid search runs BM25 and dense retrieval in parallel and fuses their ranked lists, so the system serves both exact-identifier and paraphrase queries that no single retriever covers. You cannot just add the scores, BM25 and cosine are on incomparable scales, and the larger one would dominate. Reciprocal Rank Fusion solves this by fusing on rank (sum of 1/(k+rank), k around 60), which is scale-free and automatically rewards documents both retrievers agree on, making it the safe default. Weighted score fusion is more flexible but reintroduces fragile score normalization, so prefer it only with reliable normalization and a measured reason to weight retrievers unevenly. Hybrid helps most on mixed query distributions and code/name-heavy domains, and you confirm the uplift by evaluation.
