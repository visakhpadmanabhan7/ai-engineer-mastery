LoRA & QLoRA

LoRA is why fine-tuning went from a cluster-scale luxury to something a small team does on one GPU. Understand it once, properly, and you can explain the single most practical idea in modern model adaptation, and answer the QLoRA follow-up most candidates fumble.

By the end you can
  • Explain PEFT and why training a small fraction of parameters works.
  • Describe LoRA: freeze the base, learn a low-rank update as two small matrices A and B.
  • Reason about rank r and alpha, and give a sane default starting point.
  • Explain QLoRA as LoRA on a 4-bit quantized base, and why it fits a big model on one GPU.
  • Decide when to use LoRA, when to use QLoRA, and whether to merge the adapter.

The idea: most of a fine-tune lives in a tiny subspace

Full fine-tuning updates every weight in the model, which is enormous and expensive (Day 38). The insight behind LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) is that the change you actually need to apply for one downstream task is far smaller than the model itself. The update has, in practice, low "intrinsic rank": it can be well approximated by a much smaller object. So instead of learning a full-size weight update, you learn a compact low-rank stand-in for it.

This is the heart of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT): freeze the giant pretrained model entirely, and train only a small set of new parameters bolted alongside it. You get most of the quality of full fine-tuning while training on the order of one percent of the parameters, with a fraction of the memory and a small, portable artifact at the end.

Interview-grade phrasing

"LoRA freezes the pretrained weights and learns a low-rank update beside them: for a weight matrix W, instead of training a full update ΔW, I train two small matrices A and B whose product B·A approximates ΔW. Only A and B get gradients, roughly one percent of the parameters, so I can fine-tune a large model on a single GPU and keep a tiny, swappable adapter per task." That answer hits the mechanism, the efficiency, and the deployment story in three sentences.

1 · LoRA, mechanically

Take any weight matrix in the model that you want to adapt, call it W (frozen). Full fine-tuning would learn a full-size update ΔW and use W + ΔW. LoRA refuses to materialize ΔW. Instead it factors that update into two skinny matrices:

  • A: projects the input down to a small rank r (dimensions r × d).
  • B: projects back up to full size (dimensions d × r).

The effective weight becomes W + (alpha / r) · B·A. W stays frozen; only A and B are trained. Because r is tiny (think 8, 16, 32) compared to the full dimension d (thousands), B·A has very few parameters even though it has the same shape as ΔW. That is the whole trick: a big update, represented cheaply as the product of two small matrices.

FULL FINE-TUNE LoRA (rank r) ────────────── ───────────── W is FROZEN ❄ (no gradients) input x input x ───────┬──────────────┐ │ │ │ ┌─▼─────────────┐ ┌──────────▼──────────┐ │ │ W + ΔW │ ← ΔW is │ W (frozen, d×d) │ │ (parallel path) │ (d×d, ALL │ full-size └──────────┬──────────┘ │ │ trainable) │ & trained │ ┌─────▼─────┐ A: d→r (small) └─┬─────────────┘ │ │ A │ │ │ └─────┬─────┘ ▼ │ ┌─────▼─────┐ B: r→d (small) output │ │ B │ ← only A,B trained │ └─────┬─────┘ │ (alpha/r)·(B·A) └────────►(+)◄──┘ │ ▼ output trainable params: ~100% trainable params: ~1% (just A and B)

Two consequences fall straight out of the diagram. First, memory plummets: you do not store gradients or optimizer state for W, only for the tiny A and B, which is most of the savings. Second, the adapter is a separate, tiny file. The base model is untouched, so you can train many adapters (one per task or tenant), store them cheaply, and load whichever you need on top of the same frozen base.

2 · Rank r and alpha: the two knobs

LoRA has two parameters worth understanding:

  • Rank r is the capacity of the adapter, how expressive the low-rank update is allowed to be. Higher r can capture a more complex behavior change but adds parameters and risks overfitting on small data; lower r is leaner and often plenty for narrow form/style tasks. Common starting values sit in the 8 to 32 range, and you raise r only if the task is genuinely complex and the data supports it.
  • Alpha is a scaling factor on the adapter's contribution; the update is applied as (alpha / r) · B·A, so alpha controls how strongly the learned update is injected relative to the frozen weights. A common convention is to set alpha to roughly r or 2r and treat the ratio, not the raw numbers, as the thing you tune.

The intuition to carry into an interview: r is "how much room the adapter has to learn," alpha/r is "how loud the adapter speaks." If a LoRA fine-tune underfits, raise r (more capacity) or the effective scale; if it overfits a small dataset, lower r. You are not expected to recite exact best values, but you are expected to know what each knob does and that you would sweep them on a validation set.

3 · QLoRA: fit a big model on one GPU

LoRA already shrinks the trainable parameters, but the frozen base model still has to live in GPU memory, and a large model in full precision can be too big to load at all. QLoRA closes that gap with one more move: quantize the frozen base to 4-bit precision, then run LoRA on top of it.

  • The huge base weights are loaded in a compact 4-bit form, slashing the memory just to hold the model.
  • Those base weights stay frozen; you never train in 4-bit. The trainable LoRA adapters (A and B) are kept in higher precision, and gradients only flow through them.
  • The result: you can fine-tune a model that would not even fit in full precision on a single consumer or modest cloud GPU, with quality close to higher-precision LoRA on many tasks.

The clean mental model: QLoRA = LoRA + a 4-bit frozen base. LoRA cut the cost of training parameters; QLoRA additionally cuts the cost of storing the frozen model, and together they put large-model fine-tuning within reach of one GPU.

The trade-off to name

QLoRA trades a little fidelity for a lot of memory. Quantizing the base to 4-bit can introduce small quality loss versus full or 16-bit precision, usually minor and often acceptable, but it is a real knob, not free. Reach for plain LoRA (higher-precision base) when you have the memory and want maximum quality; reach for QLoRA when the model would not otherwise fit, or when one-GPU economics matter more than the last point of quality. Knowing that QLoRA is "LoRA when you are memory-bound, at a small fidelity cost" is the senior distinction.

4 · Merging adapters (and when not to)

At serving time you have a choice. You can keep the adapter separate and apply it on top of the frozen base at inference, which is what lets you hot-swap adapters per task or per tenant on a single shared base. Or you can merge the adapter into the weights, folding (alpha/r)·B·A back into W to produce a single standalone model with no LoRA overhead and no extra inference step.

  • Merge when you are deploying one fixed behavior and want the simplest, fastest artifact (no adapter machinery at inference). Note you cannot cleanly merge a QLoRA adapter into a 4-bit base without dequantizing first.
  • Keep separate when you serve many tasks or tenants from one base and want to swap adapters dynamically, or you are still iterating and want to drop in new adapter versions without touching the base.

That swappability, many tiny adapters over one frozen base, is one of LoRA's quietly most useful properties for a multi-tenant or multi-task product.

Practice — do these before revealing

Say each answer out loud in full sentences, as if to an interviewer. Then open the solution and compare. The gap is your lesson.

Problem 1

Explain to a skeptical staff engineer why training two small matrices A and B can substitute for updating a full weight matrix, without hand-waving.

Show solution

The claim is not that A and B equal full fine-tuning; it is that the update a single downstream task requires has low intrinsic rank, so it is well approximated by a low-rank matrix, the product B·A. We keep the frozen W and add (alpha/r)·B·A as the effective update, training only A (down-projection to rank r) and B (up-projection back), which is on the order of one percent of the parameters. We do not change the model's capacity, only how much of the weight space the fine-tune is allowed to move, and empirically that small subspace captures most of the task-specific behavior. If it underfits, we raise r; that is the controlled lever, and it is far cheaper and less forgetting-prone than moving every weight.

Problem 2

Your model is too large to even load in full precision on the one GPU you have. You still want to fine-tune it. What do you reach for, what exactly changes versus plain LoRA, and what do you give up?

Show solution

Reach for QLoRA: load the frozen base in 4-bit so it fits in memory, then train LoRA adapters (kept in higher precision) on top, with gradients flowing only through the adapters, never through the quantized base. Versus plain LoRA the only change is the base precision (4-bit instead of 16-bit), which is what makes the model fit; the adapter mechanism is identical. What you give up is a small amount of fidelity from quantizing the base, usually minor and often acceptable, but a real trade-off, so you would use plain LoRA when memory allows and reserve QLoRA for the memory-bound case. Also note you cannot cleanly merge the adapter back into a 4-bit base without dequantizing.

Problem 3

You run a multi-tenant product and several tenants each want the model fine-tuned to their own tone. Why is LoRA almost ideal here, and would you merge?

Show solution

LoRA shines because the base stays frozen and each tenant's fine-tune is a tiny, separate adapter file: you store one shared base plus many small adapters instead of a full model copy per tenant, which is far cheaper, and you can load the right adapter on top of the shared base per request. You would NOT merge in this case, merging folds the adapter into the weights and produces a standalone model, which kills the swappability that makes the multi-tenant story work. Keep adapters separate and hot-swap them; merge only when you are shipping a single fixed behavior and want the leanest possible serving artifact.

Interview questions for this topic

From your Notion bank's Module 6 slice and common 2026 screens. Drill the one-line keys, then expand each into a 90-second answer with Claude using protocol prompt #2.

  • What is LoRA and why is it efficient?
    Key: freeze base weights, train small low-rank adapter matrices A and B; ~99% fewer trainable params, swappable adapters.
  • What is QLoRA?
    Key: LoRA on top of a 4-bit quantized frozen base; fits big-model fine-tuning on one GPU, at a small fidelity cost.
  • What do rank r and alpha control?
    Key: r = adapter capacity (how much it can learn); alpha/r = how strongly the update is injected; sweep on a val set, start r≈8-32.
  • Why does LoRA cut memory so much when r is just part of the story?
    Key: most savings are not storing gradients/optimizer state for the frozen base; only the tiny A and B carry optimizer state.
  • When do you keep an adapter separate vs merge it?
    Key: keep separate for multi-task/multi-tenant hot-swapping; merge for one fixed behavior and the leanest serving artifact.
  • LoRA vs QLoRA — when each?
    Key: plain LoRA when the base fits and you want max quality; QLoRA when memory-bound, accepting a small quantization quality drop.
  • What is the core assumption that makes LoRA work at all?
    Key: the task-specific weight update has low intrinsic rank, so a low-rank product B·A approximates it well.
